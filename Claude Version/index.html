About – purpose & quick learning bites

This self-assessment is a screening tool adapted from the project sources to help women/AFAB people reflect on traits commonly under-recognized in female presentations of autism. It is not diagnostic – use it to gather examples, track patterns, and prepare for a clinical conversation.

Key learning bites

What is autism?
Autism is a neurodevelopmental difference affecting social communication, sensory processing and repetitive patterns of behavior. It is lifelong and varies greatly between people.
How it can look in adults
In adults, signs can include difficulty reading emotions, high anxiety in social settings, literal interpretation of language, and strict routines – but presentation varies. Gathering childhood examples helps with assessment.
Female-presenting differences
Women and girls often 'mask' by mimicking social behaviours, using scripts, or camouflaging stimming – which makes detection harder and leads to late or missed diagnoses.
ADHD & co-occurrence
Autism and ADHD commonly co-occur; executive function and attention challenges may overlap and complicate assessment or support planning. Make sure clinicians consider both.

Reflection prompts (try writing these down)

  1. List 3 childhood examples where routines, special interests or social differences stood out.
  2. When do you feel most exhausted after social encounters? How long to recover?
  3. Have you developed scripts, rehearsed phrases, or suppressed movements/stims to fit in?
  4. Do sensory things (lights, sounds, texture) trigger strong physical reactions?
  5. Has anything changed across menstrual/hormonal cycles? (some people report fluctuations)

About scoring & staging
This tool divides screening into targeted areas: social communication, restricted/repetitive behaviour, executive function/ADHD traits, female-specific manifestations (masking, hormonal impacts), sensory/co-occurring features, burnout/energy, masking strategies, and life impact – each stage scored separately to reflect area-specific patterns. Next steps & practical guidance

If screening indicates elevated traits:
  1. Gather developmental history (childhood examples) and any school/medical reports.
  2. Take your report to your GP or a clinical psychologist experienced in adult autism assessments.
  3. Consider local support groups (AWN, NAS) and peer networks for lived-experience guidance.
Note: co-occurring ADHD is common; assessment pathways should consider both conditions together for accurate formulation.
